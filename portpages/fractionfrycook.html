Fraction Frycook

An educational kiosk and VR activity for kids to master fractions.

Team members

Paloma Fernandez, Justin Park

Timeline of project

May 2019 - June 2019 (5 weeks)

My Roles

UX/UI/Visual Design, UX Research

The Problem

Kids often find it difficult to stay engaged while learning new or difficult subjects, especially when their custom learning needs are not being attended to inside the classroom.

Needfinding

Our kiosk wasn't about fractions in the beginning! At first, we wanted to focus on other STEM subjects that relied on math, such as Physics and Chemistry. Due to this, we began our needfinding by interviewing kids between ages 12 to 16 since they are currently being exposed to these subjects.

We interviewed four kids with the following interview guide:
1. What subject(s) are you most reluctant to learn?
2. What would you change about the subject you are most reluctant to learn?
3. What learning strategy/strategies work(s) best for you?

Our participants included two 12 year olds (Annie + Julia), one 15 year old (Emily), and one 16 year old (James).

Due to time constraints for the first needfinding part, we only interviewed children since they were our target audience. After collecting feedback from our evaluators concerning this, we conducted a second round of needfinding interviews with other stakeholders (parents and teachers).

Our participants were a 3rd grade teacher (Jane), a 3rd + 4th grader, and a parent.
Key takeaways from both rounds of needfinding: The teachers' learning approaches did not completely match what kids needed in order to grasp concepts well. Kids typically are visual learners, finding it easier to learn the concepts and work out the problems if it is drawn out.

After conducting our second needfinding session, our team decided to pivot. Instead of focusing on complex subjects, we wanted to focus on fractions because they are a vital part of equations in physics, chemistry, and calculus. Having a firm grasp on fractions will assist greatly in those respective subjects.

Ideation

To guide us through our design challenge, we uncovered the following pain points and transformed them into opportunities:
- How might we encourage kids to want to learn fractions?
- How might we keep them engaged while learning fractions?
- How might we adjust to their different learning needs?
- How can we help them learn fractions using real world applications?




Our Solution

Create an engaging, immersive experience for kids to practice visually tackling fractions using real world concepts while attending to their custom learning needs. The experience will be embedded in a kiosk with a VR accessory.

User Flowchart


The kiosk itself is a math-based cooking activity. The user picks from the recipe selection menu, views the recipe and ingredients, and heads to the market to purchase those ingredients. Afterwards, the user will engage in several multiple choice questions on how to turn their ingredients into the correct amount for the required serving size, utilizing fractions. Once the user correctly fractions up their ingredients for the listed serving size, they can start physically preparing the recipe in VR.

Style Guide


Our chosen color palette felt appropriate for our target age group (7-10 year olds). Being a cooking-related product, the colors resembling fresh produce help represent the activity. Cocogoose was our selected typeface because of its rounded and playful nature, yet it holds the ability to be easily readable.

Challenges We Ran Into

As the timeline of our project was quite short, some goals could not be completed.

We were not able to interview many adult stakeholders (such as teachers and museum administrators). If allowed more time, we definitely would have gathered adequate amounts of information from older stakeholders to lead more business-oriented design decisions.

Our original design had the VR system integrated synchronously with the kiosk to allow multiple users at once, further gamifying the kiosk and encouraging direct collaboration with peers. We could not accomplish this goal due to its complex coding requirements that wouldn't be possible with our time allotment.

A physical constraint we faced was our class kiosk showcase being hosted outside. The outdoors places limitations on VR, which, along with the aforementioned time constraint, was ultimately the reason why it became an accessory instead.

What I Learned

1. How to pivot design decisions based on research under time constraints.
2. How to avoid leading questions during research.
3. Virtual Reality can greatly expand possibilities, but can be equally limited by environment.

P.S. This project received the highest grade in the class for DSGN 100. Our professor called our kiosk "one of the very best"!

Future Direction

We want to implement a level adjustment option so that users can decide how challenging they want the activity to be in order to adjust to their different learning needs. Additionally, we want to fully develop the app and integrate VR as a major component rather than an accessory to allow collaboration between co-located users and open more possibilities of it existing inside school settings and childrens' museums, such as the Discovery Cube in Los Angeles.
